## Why I Built This

Ten years ago, I became Jewish. For those unfamiliar with the process, much like being baptised or confirmed, you choose a religious name (a bit more on the significance of Hebrew names below). Most Jews don't choose their own Hebrew names – a name is given at birth and generally that remains your Hebrew name, but the name is pretty serious business. For me, at 22 years old, the process of choosing a Hebrew name was just searching figures from the bible on Wikipedia and trying to decide whether it "felt" right. I wondered how anybody naming their child could do this, knowing the effects of their choice would affect another person more than them. Wikipedia helps, of course, and I'm sure baby name websites help somewhat, but limiting the scope to Jewish thought specifically might help cut through the noise.

All that being said, the main motivation was just curiosity – what would a specifically Jewish name meanings site, powered by Sefaria (a "digital library of Jewish scriptural, theological, philosophical, legal, mystical, historical, and ethical texts"), look like?

### A Segue on Hebrew names
Your Hebrew name is used, among other times, whenever you're called to the bimah (the platform from which the Torah is read) or when others pray on your behalf (for example, if you're ill). Judaism takes names very seriously, both in law and in practice, which varies by community (eastern European (ashkenazi) Jews have a custom of not naming a baby after a living relative, while southern European and north African Jews have a custom of naming a child after either living or deceased relatives). When gravely ill, there's a custom to change or add to a person's name. 

## What It Does

Makor gives a brief description of biblical and talmudic figures and key texts in which they're mentioned.
![Project screen grab](/assets/projects/makor-screenshot.png)

## Technical Decisions

The Sefaria API provides... let's say minimal structure. I chose Python to make the response processing as easy as possible for myself. For a project of this size, I built on Flask because it's simple to set up and well-supported. I'm not making a ton of calls simultaneously, so FastAPI's threading isn't a huge gain, and I'm only making a few types of calls at the moment, so Django felt like overkill.

These are both responses to the [v2/topics](https://developers.sefaria.org/reference/get-v2-topics) endpoint. It makes sense that Joel (who's mentioned once in the Hebrew Bible) would have less relevant content on Sefaria than Moses, and the difference in response content is a classic case of needing to consider a sparse response as well as a thorough one. Beyond that, there's no guarantee a figure will have any related texts, so we need to be fault-tolerant

As a starting point, I've chosen to take build a list of texts and do a deeper contextual search later:

```python
def parse_elastic_search(search_response):
    parsed = []
    hits = search_response["hits"]["hits"]
    for hit in hits:
        category = ""
        citation = hit["_id"]
        text = hit["highlight"]["naive_lemmatizer"]
        
        if "_source" in hit:

            category = hit["_source"]["categories"][0]
            citation = hit["_source"]["ref"]
        
            if "naive_lemmatizer" in hit["_source"]:
                text = hit["_source"]["naive_lemmatizer"]
        
            else:
                text = hit["_source"]["highlight"]["naive_lemmatizer"].join(" ")
        
        section = {"source": category, "icon": "📜","references":[{"citation": citation ,"text": text}]}
        parsed.append(section)
    return parsed

```
